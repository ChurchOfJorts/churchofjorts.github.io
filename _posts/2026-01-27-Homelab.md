Throughout college and into the start of my career I never built much of a homelab past a Pi-hole server because, simply, I'd never had a need or want to. Everything I wanted to do was doable between my desktop and a couple VMs. My eyes were blind to the light until one fateful day when I went to a local security meetup. The talk of the night was a presentation of [Offensive Labs Environments (Without the Suck)](https://trustedsec.com/blog/offensive-lab-environments-without-the-suck) by Travis Kaun of TrustedSec and it introduced me to how simple, affordable, flexible, and fun a homelab could be.

## Getting started

After that, I went out into the wide world of Facebook Marketplace, found a cheap off-brand NUC PC, slapped a better SSD & RAM into it, and thought that be good. Immediately off the bat in getting the PC set-up, I found the windows installation was still domain-joined to what appeared to be a defunct company. After some wrestling with resets, BIOS, and Windows 11 OOBE I was able to get it running normally. Continuing to follow the lab guide, I set-up Hyper-V and used the AutomatedBadLabs script to try deploying a range. Unfortunately, past that is where I fell off with homelabbing. While I'm sure it was user error (and a touch of sketchy FB Marketplace computer), I had numerous issues getting a range to deploy properly. The other issue I encountered at this point was that if I forgot to leave the NUC running, or encountered an issue with Tailscale, my lab was entirely inaccessible.

## Round Two: The Network

After a hiatus, a job change, and some continued education I decided having a homelab was a pursuit I needed to revisit and this time I decided it I should start with a better foundation: a home network better than the ISP router. Here, I decided I wanted three guiding principles: small form factor, easy to manage, and cheap. Originally, I planned to try to source second-hand equipment that would more closely resemble a production network, but Facebook marketplace was not ripe and I decided that would not align with the principals of small form factor or easy to manage. From there, I pivoted to the homelab classic of Ubiquiti gear. With gear in hand I set up my VLANs, configured my VPN, and I was off to the races of homelabbing. Though with less of a focus on the lab aspect, and more on fun of setting up a mini-rack. I was lucky enough to know some folks with 3d printers, so all my equipment could fit nice & neat. 

## Back to focus: security labs
With a nicer base network in place I pivoted back to the core of the project, a security focused lab to play around in. In my research, I found out about [Ludus](https://ludus.cloud/), an open source infrastructure management tool that already had several supported ranges like Game Of Active Directory, Splunk Attack Range, and a Malware lab. I picked up a ThinkCentre m920x that would fit nicely in my mini-rack to avoid any hardware based issues that the NUC might be the source of and O installed Ludus on it. It was ruinously easy to set-up, and much faster than I could set up VMs myself. So far on it, I've tested the Splunk Attack Range and the Game Of Active Directory.

## Next Steps
Currently, I'm in no rush to move on to next steps (or spend more money) but when I am? I think a NAS server is in order for easier file access. I'd also like to investigate how I can better connect a Ludus Range to Azure or AWS for a hybrid environment for more options to experiment with logging, attack paths, and detections.
